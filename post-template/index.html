Buckshot on Bucktown, Biggie & 2Pac, and building a legacy

The Boot Camp Clik founder sits down with Mic Fox for the latest rapfabulous CONVOS — a timeline-driven conversation on origins, the golden era, and what independence really costs.

MF Mic Fox
· July 20, 2026 · 6 min read

There's a moment early in every rapfabulous CONVOS where the guest stops performing and starts remembering. For Buckshot, it came the second the conversation turned to Bucktown — not the legend, the actual block.

Over two hours with Mic Fox, the Boot Camp Clik founder traced a line from mixtape-era Brooklyn to running his own catalog decades later. This wasn't a nostalgia lap. It was a working artist explaining, step by step, how the culture actually gets built — and who gets left holding the paperwork.

The golden era, without the gloss

Ask most people about the mid-90s and you get a highlight reel. Buckshot gave something closer to a field report: the studios that doubled as classrooms, the friendships that doubled as business partnerships, and the rivalries that pushed everyone to sharpen up or fall back.

He's careful with names and generous with credit — the mark of someone who watched the era get flattened into myth and wants the record kept straight.

The culture never needed permission. It needed people willing to build the thing and own it after. — From the CONVOS conversation

Independence, and what it actually costs

The back half of the conversation gets into the part most interviews skip: ownership. What it means to control your masters, why the deal that looks best on day one rarely is, and the discipline required to bet on yourself when the advance is smaller than the offer next door.

  • Why the catalog outlasts the hit
  • The difference between "reach" and a real, connected audience
  • How a scene protects its own — and when it doesn't

It's the kind of talk that plays as well for a 19-year-old uploading beats as it does for the heads who were there. Which is, more or less, the whole point of the show.

The full mix and radio takeover are streaming now. If you only catch one thing, make it the last fifteen minutes — that's where the real gems land.
